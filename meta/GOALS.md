# Goals

As I cherry-pick different ideas from past editions and the wider family of d20 games (see [RATIONALE](RATIONALE.md)), **what exactly is the game experience I'm trying to build?**

_The simplicity of 0e meets the gritty heroic narratives of low-level 5e in a clean, modern, classless, rulings-over-rules d20 system._

## Simple

The game should have simple mechanical bones.

* There should be a handful of clean versatile core mechanics that the GM uses to adjudicate a wide range of possible situations _(rulings over rules)_
* While mechanically simple, the rules should be easily applied or adapted to a variety of different contexts with different flavor
* Rules should fade into the background during play (for example, no lookup tables; limited math and modifiers; less fumbling over rule interactions and edge cases)
* Character sheets should fit on an index card
* Monsters should follow the 0e through 2e model: Basically just HD, AC, attacks, and special abilities
* The game should be quick and easy to learn (at least to get started)
* Assuming most players are coming from traditional d20 games, design for "least surprise"

## Narrative

The game should foster dramatic moments, rich character personalities, and immersive consistency in both the world and its story.

* The current specific game world context should inform rulings, rather than the generic rules text ever trumping context-specific logic _(rulings over rules)_
* Good play should revolve around roleplaying, hard (moral) choices, character collaboration, and creative thinking, rather than optimal grid movement, careful placement of AoEs, or exploiting game rules text. (This is not a tactical skirmish game, like 4e.)
* Combat should be dangerous - something to be avoided when possible, escapable when necessary, and epic when required.
* Players should play their characters, not their character sheets. They should have a reasonable chance of trying creative solutions, rather than treating their character sheet as the menu of optimal options or buttons to push.
* Resource tracking should be easy and available when it is important to the story; it should fade into the background when it no longer important or exciting.

## Heroic

Characters should feel like gritty action heroes.

* Characters are a cut above any commoner.  They do not start as mere cannon-fodder that must survive multiple sessions to get any significant abilities.
* Yet characters are not and never become superheroes. There is always danger, darkness, death, and frailty to deal with. Characters still get knocked around, bloodied, exhausted, imprisoned, and scarred, but they press on.
* Good mental models include: Bruce Willis in _Die Hard_, Sigourney Weaver in _Aliens_, Antonio Banderas in _Desperado_
* Aim to emulate the feel of the lower two tiers of 3.5e's (gritty/heroic/wuxia/superhero) or 5e's (local hero/realm hero/realm master/world master)

## Open

The game and its characters should be flexible and hackable to support a wide range of custom tropes and settings.

* The full range of D&D monsters and adventures have always been pulled from a grab-bag of sources, from classical myth, folklore, low fantasy, high fantasy, science fantasy, space opera, and more. While a given campaign setting doesn't need to support all of those at once, the game should be easy to morph or tweak to support any of them.
* Characters should be built from modular options to allow for a range of fantasy tropes (far beyond the classic--and now stale--"four classes, four races" of so many of the retro-clones)
* It should be possible to represent a wide range of novel character concepts with mechanical substance (not only with re-skins, fluff. or flavor; even if that mechanical substance is still quite light)
* There should not be arbitrary restrictions on your options. While there is still game balance to maintain, this should be framed as investment choices to trade-off, rather than restrictions merely imposed to enforce classic tropes (such as race limitations, class caps or flavor restrictions, etc).
* It should be possible to push magic beyond the classic spell list and Vancian casting mechanic
* Lists and tables (weapons, skills, spells, etc) should be framed as a set of starting examples that is player-extensible, rather than a closed set
* The core rules should be easily tweakable with additional options, restrictions, or changes to reflect the needs of a given campaign, genre, or setting _(hackable)_

## Compatible

The game should allow easy reuse of the huge library of existing d20 content.

* The game should not have to provide (but should instead reuse) content for monsters, magic items, etc.
* The game should feel solidly a part of the D&D family, broadly defined: So somewhere in the constellation of 0e through 5.5e, all the OSR retro-clones, Pathfinder, Shadowdark, and more.
* Conversion for existing adventure modules (distances, monsters, spells, treasure, etc). should be simple enough for the GM to do on-the-fly.  Ideally, this would be possible for any OSR, 3e, or 5e adventure.
* The game may have preferred sources (such as S&W or 2e for monsters, 3.5e for magic items, etc) for which conversion is better supported.
* Conversion does not need to be exact; within about a 10% error rate of the original source material is acceptable.
